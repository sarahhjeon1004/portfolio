Autodesk Cross-Functional Program — KPI Framework

Impact HQ

🎯 Program Goal
Design and ship a reporting system that makes 53 contributors' work visible to leadership without creating comparison pressure or adding overhead.
⚠ Problem
Leadership read 40–50 individual reports every 2 weeks. 60–70% of contributors didn't know what counted as "impact." No consistent process to surface work.
👤 My Role
End-to-end program owner: stakeholder discovery, framework design, tool build, phased rollout, change management. 6–8 week delivery.
🤝 Stakeholders
Director of XD, AI/ML Research Manager, Senior Researchers, 8 Research Leads across teams

🔍 What I Found in Discovery

I ran a usability survey (70% response rate — 37 of 53 people) and conducted interviews with 5 stakeholders: leadership, researchers, and power users.

× ~19% of contributors had never submitted — the process felt unclear
× ~80% wanted access to their past submissions, but couldn't find them
× Researchers: "You want me to re-enter what I already wrote up in a doc?"
× Leadership wanted a concise digest — not a dashboard, not more meetings

🔨 What I Built
📝
Submission Framework

Three structured types — Impact, Highlight, and Challenge — each with golden examples and "no pressure" messaging. Form takes under 5 minutes.

🤖
AI Writing Coach

Stage-aware prompts that help move writing from activity to outcome framing. For strong submissions: polish and structure, not "add more impact." Guardrails against inflation.

📨
Leadership Digest

Replaced 40–50 individual reports with a concise top 3–5 per person. Added Blockers, Top Highlights, and AI-generated executive summaries.

📊
Dashboard Redesign

Removed per-person charts, counts, and averages that created comparison pressure. Reframed around themes, blockers, and celebrations.

🖥 Interactive Mockups

Four interconnected interfaces: submission form, leadership dashboard, manager digest, and published report.

Names redacted and replaced with placeholders. Data is mocked for confidentiality.

⚡ Challenges I Navigated
1
AI inflation risk

Stakeholders worried the AI coach would inflate submissions. I worked with senior researchers to set guardrails — polish and structure, not push. Credibility over impressiveness.

2
"Challenge" category pushback

People were uncomfortable flagging blockers. I reframed it as "what signal can we send so the org can help" — leadership ended up using it for tooling gaps and staffing bottlenecks.

3
Adoption resistance

The most resistant researcher became one of the most consistent contributors once I reframed submissions as "your portfolio" — not a performance metric.

💭 Reflections

What I learned: Stakeholder alignment before design prevents rework. The program succeeded because I brought leadership, contributors, and ops into scoping together. Auditing the existing system first kept me from rebuilding things that already worked.

What I'd do differently: Run user validation on the form before building v1. Define RACI earlier to avoid scope ambiguity. Launch with the "portfolio" framing from the start — it was the adoption unlock.

Autodesk Process Design & Governance Program

Research Intake Pipeline

🎯 Program Goal
Create a single intake process with data-driven prioritization — so the team can push back on low-value work with evidence, not opinion.
⚠ Problem
40+ competing requests via Slack DMs. No framework to say no. 45-minute triage debates because everything felt "urgent." Zero visibility into workload.
👤 My Role
Program owner: discovery, v1 delivery (2 weeks), v2 scoring framework, tool build, org-wide launch, change management.
🤝 Stakeholders
Research Leadership, Researchers, Product Managers, Designers
100%centralized
0lost requests
45→10 mintriage time
348projects delivered
📋 The Situation

No front door for research. PMs submitted via Slack DMs. Designers copy-pasted Confluence templates. Requests got lost, duplicated, or forgotten.

"I can't see what's coming or plan my workload"

Researchers

"I don't know where to submit"

Designers

"I never hear back on my request"

Product Managers
🚀 v1 — Solving Submission (2 weeks)

One Airtable form that auto-populates the backlog. Launched at all-hands — adoption was immediate. Requests stopped getting lost in Slack overnight.

Airtable Form
→
Team Backlog

100% of requests centralized. Zero lost. But everything still felt urgent — v1 solved submission, not prioritization.

I was transparent about that gap, and showing the plan for v2 built more trust than if v1 had been perfect.
⚙ v2 — Solving Prioritization

Key insight: instead of researchers justifying why something shouldn't happen, make the requestor justify why it should. Self-scoring on four dimensions with written rationale. Backlog auto-sorts by composite score — data arbitrates triage instead of opinion.

Business Impact1–5 + written rationale
User Impact1–5 + written rationale
Risk if Skipped1–5 + written rationale
Urgency1–5 + written rationale

⚡ Challenges I Navigated
1
PMs pushed back on self-scoring

"Why do I have to justify my own request?" But once they saw well-scored requests move faster, the complaint disappeared. The system became self-reinforcing.

2
Gaming the scores

Some requestors inflated ratings. I added written rationale as a requirement and guidance for what a 3 vs. 5 looks like. Triage team pattern-matched inflated scores easily.

3
No research manager

No one had positional authority to say "no." I embedded governance into the tool — the framework does the work of prioritization, not a person.

📊 Results
100% Request centralization
0 Lost requests
45 → 10 min Triage meeting time
348 Projects completed (FY24–26)
Triage shifted from opinion-based debates to data-driven reviews
Researchers can push back with data, not personal opinion
Low-value requests become self-evident before triage

"For the first time, I can show a PM why their request isn't the top priority without it being my personal opinion."

— Researcher
💭 Reflections

What I learned: Phased delivery builds trust faster than big-bang launches. v1 solved the visible problem (lost requests) and earned credibility to tackle the harder one (prioritization). Shipping fast and being transparent about gaps is stronger than shipping perfect.

What I'd do differently: Embed the scoring framework from day one. Add automatic status notifications — requestors shouldn't have to check manually. Build a stakeholder feedback loop into the triage cadence.

⚠ Problem
Critical data scattered across 5 disconnected systems. Hours wasted on discovery, duplicate work commissioned, access gated by tool licenses.

❓ Why This Exists

Autodesk has decades of valuable research — and almost nobody can find it. Dovetail (qual), Prism (10+ years archived), PDMS (product data), UPF Hub (User Product Feedback — NPS/survey data), Analytics. Each holds part of the picture, but they don't talk to each other.

× Access is gated — PMs, engineers, and leadership don't have Dovetail licenses
× Hours wasted on discovery — researchers manually search 5 systems to answer one question
× Duplicate research — teams commission new studies on already-studied topics
× Quant and qual disconnected — UPF Hub shows what's happening, Dovetail/Prism show why

💡 Key Findings
💡
AI is additive, not a replacement

Used for ideation and internal deliverables, not polished finals. Generated visuals informed professional work rather than replacing it.

⭐
9/11 preferred AI results

But "quality" was subjective — precision, cost, and resolution all factored in. The 2 who rated Getty higher wanted straightforward realistic imagery.

🌱
Everyone felt like a novice

Despite being early adopters, participants were still in discovery mode. Low workplace adoption and legal uncertainty reinforced this.

🔍
Prompting ≠ searching

Agency creatives used advanced techniques (URLs, chaos params). Corporate users wrote prompts that resembled search terms.

🔒
Legal shaped usage

Copyright and ownership fears kept AI use to ideation — a competitive advantage for commercially safe platforms like Getty.

⏳
Non-linear time-to-finish

A single visual: minutes to hours. A project: days to weeks — driven by stakeholder feedback loops, not the tool.

⚡ Challenges I Navigated
1
30-day engagement

Added phone screening after the pilot to vet commitment before onboarding — ensuring consistent, clean data across the full window.

2
Edits beyond the window

Set up a parallel track in UserZoomGo for participants who wouldn't edit until weeks later (waiting on client feedback).

3
No apples-to-apples

Every workflow was different — some shared detailed recordings, others static images. Each entry became its own mini-case study during synthesis.

📊 Results & Impact
12 Participants across 2 waves
30 days Observation window
9/11 Preferred AI over stock
Sept 2023 MVP launched

Findings directly informed Getty's AI Generation MVP (launched Sept 25, 2023) — shaping decisions on commercially safe generation, advanced controls (masking, outpainting), and competitive positioning against Midjourney, Firefly, and DALL-E.

💭 Reflections

What I learned: Longitudinal programs need engagement management, not just recruitment. Screening for commitment matters as much as screening for fit. Partnering closely with a senior lead taught me how to maintain program rigor when the landscape is shifting under you.

What I'd do differently: Build in weekly check-in cadences during the 30-day window to maintain engagement. Plan a post-launch wave from the start — tracking behavior change after product release closes the feedback loop.

Approach

How I run programs

01

Discover before designing

Map what people actually experience before proposing solutions. Audit existing systems so you don't rebuild what already works.

02

Align stakeholders early

Adoption comes from co-ownership, not mandates. Bring contributors into scoping so they shape the program, not just receive it.

03

Build scalable scaffolding

Frameworks and pipelines, not one-off solutions. Programs that support different teams without constant customization.

04

Embed governance in the tool

When you don't have positional authority, design the governance into the system itself. The framework does the prioritization, not a person.

05

Phase and iterate

Ship v1 fast to build trust. Be transparent about gaps. Layer on complexity once you've validated what people actually need.

06

Measure what matters

Adoption, time saved, whether the program changes decisions. Track outcomes, not just outputs.

How I measure program success

Delivery

On-time execution, adoption rates, cycle throughput, time savings for stakeholders

Sustainability

Consistent, scalable, not dependent on one person — playbooks and systems that outlast you

Stakeholder trust

Leadership sees value. Teams see clarity. Cross-functional partners trust the process.

The real test: does the program reduce burden and improve decision-making — or does it just create more process?
