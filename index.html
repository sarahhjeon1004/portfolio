Autodesk KPI Framework & Visibility System

Impact HQ

53 people doing great work with no consistent way to show it. I built the visibility system that changed that — from discovery through rollout.

My Role & Collaboration

ToolsAirtable, Slack, AI prompts
Scope53 contributors

  • Designed the KPI framework and submission types

  • Wrote the AI writing coach prompts and calibration guardrails

8 Research Leads Phase 2 validation through "take back to managers" sessions
The Situation

PSET XD had 53 active contributors shipping work across 22 bi-weekly reporting cycles — but leadership had no consistent way to see what was landing and what was blocked. Every two weeks, leadership was reading 40–50 individual reports just to figure out the big picture. Meanwhile, 60–70% of contributors were confused about what "impact" even meant, and only 30–40% were satisfied with the submission process.

53 Active contributors
60-70% Confused about "impact"
40-50 Reports leadership read every 2 weeks
What I Found in Discovery

I ran a usability survey (70% response rate — 37 of 53 people) and conducted interviews with 5 stakeholders: leadership, researchers, and power users. The findings shaped every decision I made:

× ~19% of contributors had never submitted — not because they had nothing to share, but because the process felt unclear
× ~80% wanted access to their past submissions, but couldn't find them
× Researchers said: "You want me to re-enter what I already wrote up in a doc?"
× Leadership wanted a concise digest — not a dashboard, not more metrics, not more meetings
What I Built
Submission Framework

Three structured types — Impact, Highlight, and Challenge — each with golden examples and "no pressure" messaging. Form takes under 5 minutes.

AI Writing Coach

Stage-aware prompts that help move writing from activity to outcome framing. For strong submissions: polish and structure, not "add more impact." Guardrails against inflation.

Leadership Digest

Replaced 40–50 individual reports with a concise top 3–5 per person. Added Blockers, Top Highlights, and AI-generated executive summaries.

Dashboard Redesign

Removed per-person charts, counts, and averages that created comparison pressure. Reframed around themes, blockers, and celebrations.

Challenges I Navigated
1
AI inflation risk

Stakeholders worried the AI coach would inflate submissions. I worked with senior researchers to set guardrails — polish and structure, not push. Credibility over impressiveness.

2
"Challenge" category pushback

People were uncomfortable flagging blockers. I reframed it as "what signal can we send so the org can help" — leadership ended up using it for tooling gaps and staffing bottlenecks.

3
Adoption resistance

The most resistant researcher became one of the most consistent contributors once I reframed submissions as "your portfolio" — not a performance metric.

Results
264 Impact submissions across 22 cycles
81% Participation rate (up from ~81% baseline)
<5 min Form completion time
2-3 hrs Weekly maintenance (system runs itself)
Leadership reads a concise digest instead of 40–50 reports
Comparison pressure removed — no per-person metrics
Teams started celebrating each other's work
38% became consistent contributors across cycles
Reflections

What I learned: Start stakeholder alignment before designing. The system succeeded because leadership, researchers, and design ops all shaped it — not because I designed it in isolation. Golden examples from day one would have accelerated adoption. And auditing the existing system first saved me from rebuilding things that already worked.

What I'd do differently: I would have done user research on the form itself before building v1 — validated what "low-friction" actually means to researchers. I'd launch with a simpler form and the "portfolio" framing from the start, rather than discovering that framing partway through. And I'd define collaboration roles earlier to avoid scope ambiguity with design ops.

Autodesk Intake & Prioritization System

Research Intake Pipeline

40+ competing research requests, no front door, and 45-minute triage debates. I built a system where data decides priority — not politics.

My Role & Collaboration
Timelinev1 in ~2 weeks, v2 a few weeks later
ToolsAirtable (form, Kanban, automations)
Scope40+ competing requests
What I owned
  • Ran discovery interviews with researchers, designers, and PMs
  • Designed and built v1 intake form + auto-populated backlog
  • Designed v2 self-scoring prioritization framework
  • Built Airtable schema, automations, and triage playbook
  • Launched at all-hands — immediate adoption
  • Documented triage process for research leadership
Who I worked with

Researchers Discovery — surfaced workload visibility and inability to push back

Designers Discovery — highlighted confusion about where to submit requests
The Situation

There was no front door for research. PMs submitted requests through Slack DMs. Designers copy-pasted Confluence templates. Requests got lost, duplicated, or forgotten. Researchers had 40+ competing requests and no framework to prioritize or push back. Triage meetings devolved into 45-minute debates because every request felt equally "urgent."

v1 — Solving Submission (2 weeks)

I started with the simplest version of the fix: one Airtable form that auto-populates the team backlog. I launched it at all-hands and adoption was immediate — requests stopped getting lost in Slack overnight.

Airtable Form
→
Team Backlog

100% of requests were now centralized. Zero lost requests. But researchers were still overwhelmed — everything was in one place, and everything still felt urgent.

v1 solved submission but not prioritization. I was transparent about that, and showing the plan for v2 built more trust than if v1 had been perfect.
v2 — Solving Prioritization

The key insight: instead of researchers justifying why something shouldn't happen, make the requestor justify why it should. I designed a self-scoring framework where requestors rate their own request on four dimensions with written rationale. The backlog auto-sorts by composite score — and data arbitrates triage instead of opinion.

Business Impact 1 – 5 + written rationale
User Impact 1 – 5 + written rationale
Risk if Skipped 1 – 5 + written rationale
Urgency 1 – 5 + written rationale

Challenges I Navigated
1
PMs pushed back on self-scoring

"Why do I have to justify my own request?" But once they saw well-scored requests move faster, the complaint disappeared. The system became self-reinforcing.

2
Gaming the scores

Some requestors inflated their ratings. I added written rationale as a requirement and guidance examples for what a 3 vs. 5 looks like. The triage team pattern-matched inflated scores easily.

3
No research manager

There was no one with positional authority to say "no." I embedded governance into the tool — the framework does the work of prioritization, not a person.

Results
100% Request centralization
0 Lost requests
45 → 10 min Triage meeting time
348 Projects completed (FY24–26)
Triage shifted from opinion-based debates to data-driven reviews
Researchers can push back with data, not personal opinion
Low-value requests become self-evident before triage
Requestors link existing research before asking for new work

"For the first time, I can show a PM why their request isn't the top priority without it being my personal opinion."

— Researcher
Reflections

What I learned: Solve the system, not the symptom. v1 fixed submission — but that only moved the bottleneck to prioritization. Shipping v1 fast built the trust that made v2 possible. Being transparent about what isn't working builds more credibility than trying to make v1 perfect.

What I'd do differently: I'd embed the scoring framework from day one instead of shipping a simpler v1 first. The two-phase approach built trust, but it also meant researchers dealt with an unsolved prioritization problem for weeks. I'd also add an automatic feedback loop — right now requestors don't know where their request stands unless they check the backlog. A Slack notification when status changes would close that gap.

Autodesk AI-Powered Research Intelligence

Ask Me Anything

Decades of research locked in silos that don't talk to each other. I'm building the AI-powered system that makes it all searchable — for everyone.

My Role & Collaboration
StatusPrototype in development
ToolsDovetail API, MCP, Slack, Node.js
SeekingEngineers & designers

  • Built MCP server with Dovetail API integration
  • Designed the UI modeled after enterprise platforms like Stravito
  • Defined personas, information architecture, and integration strategy
Who I worked with

Researchers AI workshops on synthesis — identified synthesis support as highest-value use case
Internal MCP Community Aligned on open protocol standards and shared learnings across AI tool teams
Why This Exists

Autodesk has decades of valuable research — and almost nobody can find it. Usability studies live in Dovetail (qualitative research platform). 10+ years of archived research sits in Prism (internal research repository). Customer data is in PDMS (product data management research). Sentiment and trend data lives in UPF Hub (User Product Feedback — quantitative survey and NPS data). Each holds part of the picture, but they don't talk to each other. The research practice has been trying to unlock these repositories for years.

× Access is gated — PMs, engineers, and leadership don't have Dovetail licenses, so they're locked out of qualitative data entirely
× Hours wasted on discovery — researchers manually search and cross-reference across 5 disconnected systems to answer a single question
× Duplicate research — teams commission new studies on already-studied topics because there's no way to know what already exists
× Quant and qual are disconnected — UPF Hub (sentiment data) can tell you what's happening, but Dovetail/Prism tell you why — and there's no way to connect them
Who It's For

This isn't just a tool for researchers. Each role has different questions, different access levels, and different workflows. The platform adapts to all of them with role-based quick starts and persona-aware search.

Researcher

Find existing studies before starting new research. Avoid duplication and build on prior work.

Product Manager

Validate decisions with research evidence. Track feature requests and customer sentiment.

Designer

Find customer quotes and usability findings to ground design decisions in real feedback.

Engineer

See customer pain points and bug feedback for your area. Understand the "why" behind priorities.

Leadership

Get high-level themes, sentiment trends, and research ROI without digging through tools.

Strategy & Ops

Cross-reference quantitative and qualitative signals to spot patterns across products.

What I'm Building

A research intelligence platform that unifies all 5 sources — Dovetail (342+ items), Prism (1,200+ studies), PDMS (89 items), and Analytics (56 dashboards) — into one searchable interface. AI summarizes findings across sources, cites the originals, and delivers answers through Slack, Confluence, and a Chrome extension — so people get research where they already work.

Meeting People Where They Work

The platform extends into daily workflows — because the biggest barrier to research adoption isn't quality, it's accessibility.

Target Impact
2,450+ Research items searchable
5 Connected sources
hrs → min Research discovery time
All roles Access democratized
Reflections

What I've learned so far: Building on open protocols (MCP) instead of proprietary UIs avoids vendor lock-in and gives us flexibility as the AI landscape changes. The biggest unlock isn't the technology — it's meeting people in their existing tools (Slack, Confluence) instead of asking them to learn a new platform. AI workshops with researchers showed me that synthesis support is the highest-value use case, not just search.

What I'm watching for: The risk is building a tool that's technically impressive but doesn't change behavior. I need to validate early with real usage data — are people actually making decisions differently because of this, or just searching for fun? I'd also want to measure whether duplicate research requests actually decrease after rollout, not just assume they will.

How I think about research operations

01

Journey-map first

Build from what people actually experience, not assumptions. Audit the existing system before designing in a vacuum.

02

Co-design for adoption

Adoption comes from ownership, not mandates. The people being measured should help define the metrics.

03

Standardize the scaffolding

Templates and pipelines, not prescriptive methods. Systems that support different research approaches.

04

Embed governance in the tool

When you don't have positional authority, design the governance into the system.

05

Phase and iterate

Ship v1 fast to build trust. Layer on complexity once you know what people actually need.

06

Measure what matters

Adoption, time-to-insight, whether findings reach decisions. Does the system reduce burden?

How I measure success

Metrics

Adoption rates, participation, digest cycles, time-to-insight, research throughput

Governance

Consistent, scalable, not dependent on one person — playbooks that outlast you

Stakeholder satisfaction

Leadership sees value. Researchers see clarity. Partners trust the system.

The real test: does the system reduce burden and make research more impactful — or does it just create more work?
